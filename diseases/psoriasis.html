What is Psoriasis?

Psoriasis is a chronic autoimmune condition that causes skin cells to build up rapidly on the surface of the skin. This buildup leads to thick, red patches with silvery scales, which are often itchy or painful. Psoriasis is a long-term (chronic) condition that can last for months or years, with periods of flare-ups and remission.

Symptoms of Psoriasis

  • Red, raised, inflamed patches of skin
  • Silver or white scales on the skin's surface
  • Dry, cracked skin that may bleed
  • Itching, burning, or soreness
  • Thickened or ridged nails
  • Swollen or stiff joints (in cases of psoriatic arthritis)

Causes of Psoriasis

The exact cause of psoriasis is not fully understood, but it is believed to be related to a combination of genetic and environmental factors. The immune system mistakenly attacks healthy skin cells, causing the rapid skin cell turnover that leads to psoriasis. Some common triggers include:

  • Infections, such as strep throat or skin infections
  • Injury to the skin, like cuts, scrapes, or sunburn
  • Stress
  • Medications, such as lithium or certain blood pressure medications
  • Weather changes, particularly cold, dry weather

Treating Psoriasis

While there is no cure for psoriasis, there are treatments available to help manage symptoms and reduce flare-ups. Treatment plans depend on the severity of the condition, but may include:

  • Topical treatments (e.g., corticosteroids, vitamin D analogs, topical retinoids)
  • Light therapy (UVB light therapy)
  • Systemic medications (oral or injectable drugs that suppress the immune system)
  • Biologic therapies (targeting specific parts of the immune system)

Managing Psoriasis

Living with psoriasis requires ongoing management, which includes not only medical treatments but also lifestyle adjustments. Here are some tips to help manage the condition:

  • Moisturize your skin regularly to prevent dryness and irritation
  • Avoid triggers like stress, smoking, and excessive alcohol consumption
  • Take short, lukewarm baths to soothe the skin
  • Follow a healthy diet to maintain overall health
  • Consult with a dermatologist for personalized treatment plans
